MRR vs Revenue Quality

$50K MRR sounds great — until you learn 15% is zombie revenue, 30% is concentrated in three customers, and 40% is on annual plans renewing next quarter. Here's how to separate vanity MRR from durable revenue.

Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR) is the headline number every SaaS seller quotes. It's also the number most likely to mislead a buyer. MRR tells you how much revenue is billed this month — it says nothing about how much of that revenue will still exist in 6, 12, or 18 months. Revenue quality is the answer to that question.

Why MRR Is a Vanity Metric

MRR counts every active subscription at its current billing rate. It makes no distinction between:

  • A customer who logged in today and is actively expanding their usage
  • A customer who hasn't opened the product in 90 days but hasn't cancelled yet
  • A customer who represents 25% of total MRR and could leave at any time
  • A customer on a legacy discounted plan paying 60% less than market rate

All four show up identically in the MRR number. But they represent vastly different levels of revenue durability.

The Components of Revenue Quality

Revenue quality is a composite assessment of how durable and trustworthy the MRR number is. It accounts for five factors:

  1. Gross Revenue Retention (GRR): What percentage of MRR from period N is still present in period N+12, excluding expansion? This is the floor — the worst-case retention scenario. Healthy SaaS: 85-95%.
  2. Net Revenue Retention (NRR): Same as GRR but including expansion revenue. Above 100% means existing customers are growing faster than churn. This is the gold standard metric.
  3. Concentration-adjusted retention: GRR/NRR adjusted for customer concentration risk. If your top customer represents 30% of MRR, effective retention is lower because their departure has outsized impact.
  4. Engagement-weighted MRR: MRR weighted by product engagement levels. Inactive accounts (zombie MRR) are discounted.
  5. Expansion revenue sustainability: Is expansion coming from genuine upsell (more seats, higher tier) or from price increases that customers may resist at renewal?

Net Revenue Retention: The Metric That Matters

NRR is the single best summary metric for revenue quality. It measures what happened to the revenue from customers you had 12 months ago:

NRR = (Starting MRR + Expansion MRR − Churned MRR − Contraction MRR) / Starting MRR

The Expansion Revenue Trap

Expansion revenue (upsells, seat additions, plan upgrades) can make NRR look great while masking underlying problems. Watch for:

  • Price-driven expansion: Revenue grows because prices increased, not because customers gained value. This is unsustainable — eventually customers churn due to price.
  • Seat inflation: A large enterprise customer keeps adding seats — but those seats are unused. This is just delayed concentration risk.
  • Forced upgrades: Customers upgraded because old tiers were deprecated. They may not renew at the new price.

Genuine expansion comes from customers getting more value and naturally scaling their usage. Check whether expansion correlates with engagement growth — if MRR is rising but login frequency isn't, the expansion may be artificial.

Durable MRR: The Number You Should Value

After adjusting for all quality factors, you arrive at durable MRR — the revenue you can reasonably expect to persist for 12+ months post-acquisition:

Durable MRR = Total MRR − Zombie MRR − Concentration Risk Buffer − Annual Cliff Exposure

This is the number that should drive your acquisition valuation. It's typically 70-90% of headline MRR for healthy SaaS, and can be as low as 50-60% for problematic targets. For the full scoring methodology, see our Revenue Quality Score guide.
