How to Evaluate a SaaS Before Buying

A six-step buyer-side framework for evaluating any SaaS acquisition target. Move from headline metrics to durable revenue analysis — the difference between a good deal and a value trap.

Evaluating a SaaS business for acquisition starts with one question: can you trust the revenue number? Most buyers start with the seller's P&L and work backward — that's backwards. Start with raw subscription data: customer-level MRR by month, plan assignments, last-login dates, and contract terms. This framework walks through the six analyses that separate healthy, durable revenue from fragile numbers that will collapse post-close.

TL;DR: Evaluating a SaaS acquisition involves six analyses: revenue quality (separate durable MRR from zombie MRR), logo and revenue churn (always recalculate from raw data), concentration risk (compute HHI), cohort retention decay (compare older vs newer cohorts), annual plan cliff exposure (check renewal concentrations), and NRR trends (declining NRR means the revenue base is rotting from within).

1Revenue Quality Assessment

Start by separating durable revenue from fragile revenue. Headline MRR is not durable revenue. Durable revenue is MRR from customers who are actively using the product, on appropriate plans, with healthy engagement patterns.

Key questions at this stage:

  • What percentage of MRR comes from actively engaged customers?
  • How much MRR is from inactive paid accounts (zombie MRR)?
  • Is expansion revenue growing or shrinking as a percentage of total?
  • Are there pricing cliffs where customers on legacy plans pay dramatically less than new customers?

For a deep dive, read our MRR vs Revenue Quality analysis and the Revenue Quality Score methodology.

2Churn Analysis (Logo vs. Revenue)

Churn is the single most important metric in SaaS valuation, but sellers routinely present it in the most flattering light. You need both logo churn (percentage of customers who cancel) and revenue churn (percentage of MRR lost), measured separately:

  • Logo churn tells you breadth of customer dissatisfaction
  • Revenue churn tells you financial impact of departures
  • When revenue churn exceeds logo churn, your largest customers are leaving — a concentration signal
  • When logo churn exceeds revenue churn, small customers leave but expansion revenue masks the loss

Benchmark against industry-specific churn rates — a 3% monthly churn means very different things in enterprise SaaS vs. SMB e-commerce apps. Also read about the 7 hidden churn tricks sellers use.

3Customer Concentration Check

Concentration risk determines whether the loss of a single customer is a survivable event or a business-ending one. Calculate the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) from the subscription CSV and identify any "whale" customers above 5% of MRR.

Red flags at this stage:

  • Top 3 customers exceed 40% of MRR
  • Top 10 customers exceed 60% of MRR
  • HHI above 1,800 (highly concentrated)
  • Whale customers on monthly contracts with no lock-in

Full methodology in our revenue concentration risk guide and the related customer concentration risk analysis.

4Annual Plan & Renewal Risk

If more than 50% of MRR is on annual contracts, monthly churn metrics are heavily distorted. You need to map the renewal calendar:

  • When do annual contracts expire?
  • How much MRR is exposed in any given month?
  • What's the historical annual renewal rate?
  • Are there renewal cliffs where multiple contracts expire simultaneously?

See our annual plan churn risk guide for the full framework.

5Cohort Analysis

Cohort analysis reveals whether customer quality is improving or deteriorating over time. Group customers by signup month and track their revenue retention curve:

  • Are newer cohorts retaining better or worse than older ones?
  • Is there a "cohort cliff" at month 3, 6, or 12 where retention drops sharply?
  • Do cohorts from specific acquisition channels perform differently?
  • Is expansion revenue within cohorts growing (good) or flat/declining (concerning)?

Declining cohort retention is one of the strongest acquisition red flags — it means the product is losing product-market fit, even if headline growth still looks positive due to new customer acquisition.

6Growth Sustainability Audit

Finally, assess whether the growth trajectory is sustainable. Key signals:

  • New MRR trend: Is new customer acquisition growing, flat, or declining?
  • CAC dependency: How much of growth depends on paid acquisition vs. organic/word-of-mouth?
  • Net revenue retention: Is NRR above 100% (expansion exceeds churn) or below?
  • Channel concentration: Does one acquisition channel drive most new signups?

A business growing 5% month-over-month with negative net revenue retention is fragile — it's growing despite losing existing revenue. A business with flat MRR but 110% NRR is durable — it's retaining and expanding even without new acquisition.

Putting It Together: The Go/No-Go Decision

After completing all six analyses, you should be able to answer one question with confidence: What is the durable revenue base, and what multiple of it am I willing to pay?

Durable revenue = Total MRR − Zombie MRR − Concentration-adjusted risk buffer − Annual cliff exposure. Only this number should drive your valuation, not the headline MRR the seller quotes.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is hidden churn in a SaaS acquisition?

Hidden churn is revenue decay that headline metrics conceal: customers on annual plans who have already stopped using the product, paid accounts sitting inactive, or revenue concentrated in a few logos about to leave. A SaaS business can show flat MRR while its real retention is collapsing. ChurnLens surfaces these signals before you buy, so you price the deal on true revenue quality.

How does ChurnLens score revenue quality?

ChurnLens analyzes five dimensions: revenue concentration, logo retention, annual-plan churn risk, inactive paid accounts, and MRR decline patterns. Each is weighted into a single 0-100 revenue-quality score benchmarked against comparable SaaS businesses. The score tells an acquirer whether reported MRR is durable or propped up by customers who are one renewal away from leaving, all before the deal closes.

Why do SaaS acquirers need due diligence on churn?

Purchase price is usually a multiple of recurring revenue, so overstated retention directly inflates what you pay. A business with 20% hidden annual-plan churn is worth far less than its MRR implies. Buyers who skip churn diligence discover the decay only after closing, when it is too late to renegotiate. ChurnLens gives that visibility during the evaluation window instead.

What red flags should I check before buying a SaaS business?

Watch for revenue concentrated in a handful of accounts, a widening gap between signups and active users, annual contracts that never renew, and MRR that grows only through discounting. Each pattern signals fragile revenue. ChurnLens automatically flags these red flags from uploaded revenue data and ranks them by how much they threaten the durability of the recurring revenue base.

Key terms, defined

Revenue concentration
The share of total revenue coming from the largest customers — high concentration is a churn and valuation risk.
Logo retention
The percentage of customers (logos) retained over a period, independent of expansion revenue.
Net revenue retention (NRR)
Revenue retained from existing customers including expansion and contraction, expressed as a percentage.
