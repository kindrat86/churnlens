The 5-Risk Buyer-Side Method

Every risk a seller can hide — structured into a named framework. Not tips. A system. Read the full method →

01

The Churn Divergence Detector

Logo churn and revenue churn, computed separately and compared. Losing 5% of logos is fine. Losing 5% of logos representing 40% of MRR is a dealbreaker. The divergence IS the signal.

02

The Concentration Vulnerability Index

Flags when 3 customers represent 60%+ of MRR. If any of those churn post-close, your "SaaS" becomes a professional services firm overnight. Index scored, not just flagged.

03

The Annual-Plan Decay Projection

Identifies customers locked into annual plans who are statistically likely to cancel at renewal. The quietest revenue killer — invisible until renewal season hits post-close.

04

The Zombie MRR Detector

Detects customers still paying but not engaging. Zombie MRR looks stable on the P&L but disappears one invoice at a time. The seller never flags this — it makes their number look good.

05

The Revenue Quality Scorecard

A composite A–F grade weighting retention, concentration, expansion revenue, and growth trends. One letter grade tells you more than a 50-page CIM about whether this MRR will still be here in 12 months.

06

The MRR Trajectory Forensics

Shows whether MRR is trending up, flat, or silently decaying. A flat headline MRR often hides declining cohorts being masked by new sales. We separate the two so you see what's real.

FALSE BELIEF #1 — The Vehicle
"The seller's churn methodology is industry-standard, so the number is reliable."
Truth: There is no standard. Every seller makes methodology choices that flatter their number. The only reliable number is the one you compute from raw data. (4.2x average gap between reported and real.)
FALSE BELIEF #2 — Internal Belief
"I'm not technical enough to analyze raw subscription data myself."
Truth: You don't need to be. You need the CSV and a tool that computes churn five ways. The analysis takes minutes, not a finance degree. Your "I'm not technical" belief is exactly what sellers count on.
FALSE BELIEF #3 — External Belief
"The seller wouldn't hide churn — they'd get caught in the earnout."
Truth: Most churn methodology choices are legal, defensible, and invisible. Earnouts don't protect you from legitimate-looking accounting that understates real churn by 4x. The earnout catches fraud, not methodology.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is hidden churn in a SaaS acquisition?

Hidden churn is revenue decay that headline metrics conceal: customers on annual plans who have already stopped using the product, paid accounts sitting inactive, or revenue concentrated in a few logos about to leave. A SaaS business can show flat MRR while its real retention is collapsing. ChurnLens surfaces these signals before you buy, so you price the deal on true revenue quality.

How does ChurnLens score revenue quality?

ChurnLens analyzes five dimensions: revenue concentration, logo retention, annual-plan churn risk, inactive paid accounts, and MRR decline patterns. Each is weighted into a single 0-100 revenue-quality score benchmarked against comparable SaaS businesses. The score tells an acquirer whether reported MRR is durable or propped up by customers who are one renewal away from leaving, all before the deal closes.

Why do SaaS acquirers need due diligence on churn?

Purchase price is usually a multiple of recurring revenue, so overstated retention directly inflates what you pay. A business with 20% hidden annual-plan churn is worth far less than its MRR implies. Buyers who skip churn diligence discover the decay only after closing, when it is too late to renegotiate. ChurnLens gives that visibility during the evaluation window instead.

What red flags should I check before buying a SaaS business?

Watch for revenue concentrated in a handful of accounts, a widening gap between signups and active users, annual contracts that never renew, and MRR that grows only through discounting. Each pattern signals fragile revenue. ChurnLens automatically flags these red flags from uploaded revenue data and ranks them by how much they threaten the durability of the recurring revenue base.

Key terms, defined

Revenue concentration
The share of total revenue coming from the largest customers — high concentration is a churn and valuation risk.
Logo retention
The percentage of customers (logos) retained over a period, independent of expansion revenue.
Net revenue retention (NRR)
Revenue retained from existing customers including expansion and contraction, expressed as a percentage.
