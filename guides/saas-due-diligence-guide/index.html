The revenue-quality due-diligence framework

Most SaaS acquisitions fail because the acquirer evaluated the business on top-line metrics (MRR, growth rate, ARPU) while ignoring the revenue-quality signals underneath. ChurnLens's due-diligence framework evaluates five dimensions that determine whether the revenue is durable or fragile:

1. Revenue ConcentrationWhat percentage of MRR comes from the top 1, 3, and 5 customers? If >30% from top 3, a single cancellation destroys the acquisition thesis.
2. Logo Retention (not just dollar retention)Net dollar retention can mask logo churn. If you're losing 15% of customers annually but the survivors are expanding, NDR looks healthy — but the business is bleeding.
3. Annual-Plan Churn RiskWhat percentage of revenue is on annual contracts up for renewal in the next 90 days? A concentration of renewals in one quarter is a ticking clock.
4. Inactive Paid AccountsHow many paying customers haven't logged in for 30+ days? These are the "zombie MRR" accounts that churn silently when the credit card expires.
5. MRR Decline PatternIs MRR growing, flat, or declining when you factor out expansions? A business that adds $10K/mo in new MRR but loses $12K/mo to churn is shrinking — even if headline MRR is up from last year's expansions.

Red flags that kill deals

Over 60% of SaaS acquisitions that fail within 18 months share one of these patterns: (1) the top customer represents >25% of revenue and churns within 6 months of close; (2) "healthy" NDR of 110%+ masks logo churn of 20%+ — the business is a leaky bucket with a few big expanders; (3) >20% of accounts are inactive but paying, meaning the churn hasn't happened yet but is baked in.
