Why legal tech SaaS churn is different

Legal-technology and practice-management saas exhibits a distinct churn signature that generic SaaS benchmarks misread. The dominant pattern is firm-consolidation churn masked by sticky multi-year contracts. Law firms and legal-ops teams are the typical buyer profile, and their churn behavior is shaped by industry-specific triggers: 78% of revenue sits on annual plans (so reported churn lags real decay by a full renewal cycle), and MRR volatility is low (so a single quarter's snapshot can flatter or slander the true trajectory).

The result is that legal tech SaaS sellers can present clean-looking retention slides — gross revenue churn "under 2.3%", logo retention "above 97%" — while the underlying business is hollowing out. ChurnLens is built to find the hollowing.

The decay signals ChurnLens tracks for legal tech SaaS

  • Revenue concentration: what share of MRR sits in the top-5 logos? For legal-technology and practice-management SaaS, concentration above 35% is a structural risk that surfaces as sudden, lumpy churn.
  • Inactive paid accounts: paying customers (law firms and legal-ops teams) whose product usage has dropped below the activation threshold. These are logos that have already churned in behavior — they just haven't churned in billing yet.
  • Annual-plan renewal risk: with 78% annual share, ChurnLens projects which cohorts are scheduled to renew in the next 1–4 quarters and models the downside case.
  • MRR decline patterns: low MRR volatility means ChurnLens looks at trailing 6-month weighted averages, not the most recent month.
  • Cohort retention curves: whether the 2.8% logo churn is concentrated in old cohorts (a maturing product problem) or new cohorts (an onboarding/ICP problem).
