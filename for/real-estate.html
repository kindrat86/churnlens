Why real estate SaaS churn is different

Real-estate and brokerage saas exhibits a distinct churn signature that generic SaaS benchmarks misread. The dominant pattern is market-cycle churn — agent seat counts collapse when transactions drop. Brokerages and agent teams are the typical buyer profile, and their churn behavior is shaped by industry-specific triggers: 55% of revenue sits on annual plans (so reported churn lags real decay by a full renewal cycle), and MRR volatility is high (so a single quarter's snapshot can flatter or slander the true trajectory).

The result is that real estate SaaS sellers can present clean-looking retention slides — gross revenue churn "under 3.5%", logo retention "above 94%" — while the underlying business is hollowing out. ChurnLens is built to find the hollowing.

The decay signals ChurnLens tracks for real estate SaaS

  • Revenue concentration: what share of MRR sits in the top-5 logos? For real-estate and brokerage SaaS, concentration above 35% is a structural risk that surfaces as sudden, lumpy churn.
  • Inactive paid accounts: paying customers (brokerages and agent teams) whose product usage has dropped below the activation threshold. These are logos that have already churned in behavior — they just haven't churned in billing yet.
  • Annual-plan renewal risk: with 55% annual share, ChurnLens projects which cohorts are scheduled to renew in the next 1–4 quarters and models the downside case.
  • MRR decline patterns: high MRR volatility means ChurnLens looks at trailing 6-month weighted averages, not the most recent month.
  • Cohort retention curves: whether the 5.0% logo churn is concentrated in old cohorts (a maturing product problem) or new cohorts (an onboarding/ICP problem).

Frequently asked questions

What is a healthy logo churn rate for a real estate SaaS?

For real-estate and brokerage SaaS, a defensible logo churn rate benchmark is around 5.0% per month. ChurnLens flags targets above this band and, more importantly, looks for the gap between reported logo churn and the underlying decay signals (inactive paid accounts, declining product usage, expiring annual plans) that show the real trajectory.
