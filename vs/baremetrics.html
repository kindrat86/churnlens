ChurnLens vs Baremetrics

Short answer: they aren't really competitors — they serve opposite readers. Baremetrics is a live dashboard for the operator running a subscription business. ChurnLens is a due-diligence lens for the buyer evaluating that business before acquiring it. If you're running a SaaS, you want Baremetrics. If you're about to buy one, you want ChurnLens.

Both tools read subscription revenue data — MRR, churn, retention — so they look similar at a glance. The difference is the question each is built to answer. Baremetrics answers "how is my business doing this month?" ChurnLens answers "is this business I'm about to buy as healthy as its dashboard makes it look?" Same raw numbers; completely different job.

What Baremetrics does well

Baremetrics is a mature, well-loved operator dashboard used by 900+ companies. If you run a subscription business, it's excellent at the day-to-day.

  • Control Center & Smart Dashboards — one live view of MRR, churn, LTV, and month-over-month movement
  • Recover — dunning that wins back failed payments automatically
  • Cancellation Insights & Segmentation — why customers leave, sliced by cohort and plan
  • Deep billing integrations — plugs straight into Stripe, Chargebee, Paddle, and more, with forecasting on top

Where ChurnLens is the right tool instead

ChurnLens exists for the moment Baremetrics isn't built for: someone else's numbers, under acquisition scrutiny. A healthy-looking MRR chart can hide the three risks that sink SaaS deals, and ChurnLens is built to surface them.

  • Revenue concentration — how much of MRR rides on a handful of accounts (a top-heavy book is a valuation risk a growth dashboard won't flag)
  • Logo-retention quality — is net revenue held up by a few expansions masking broad logo churn?
  • Annual-plan cliff risk — annual contracts that inflate current MRR but are about to lapse
  • MRR-decline patterns — the trend underneath the headline number, framed for a go/no-go decision
Honest note: ChurnLens is not a Baremetrics replacement for operators. It has no dunning, no live ops tooling, and isn't meant to run a business day to day. It's a diligence lens. If you're the founder, keep Baremetrics; if you're the buyer, add ChurnLens.

When to choose each

Choose ChurnLens if…

  • You're evaluating a SaaS to acquire and need revenue quality pressure-tested, not just charted
  • You care about concentration, retention quality, and annual-plan risk for a go/no-go call
  • You want a diligence-ready read you can hand to an investment committee

Choose Baremetrics if…

  • You operate a subscription business and want a live metrics dashboard
  • You need dunning/recovery and ongoing churn and forecasting tooling
  • You want turnkey integrations with your billing stack

Our honest verdict

Baremetrics is great for founders running the business. ChurnLens is for the buyer evaluating those founders. If a deal is on the table, the two are complementary — the seller's Baremetrics tells the story, and ChurnLens checks whether the story holds.

Frequently asked questions

Is ChurnLens a Baremetrics alternative?

Not really — they serve opposite readers. Baremetrics is a live analytics dashboard for the operator running a subscription business; ChurnLens is a due-diligence lens for a buyer evaluating that business before acquiring it.

Can I just point Baremetrics at an acquisition target?

Only if the seller gives you live access to their billing stack — and even then, Baremetrics is built to help operators run a business, not to score acquisition risk. It reports MRR, churn, and forecasts; it doesn't frame concentration, logo-retention quality, or annual-plan cliff risk the way a buyer needs. ChurnLens is built around exactly those questions.

Do they work together?

Yes. A seller commonly runs Baremetrics for day-to-day metrics, and a buyer runs ChurnLens over the same underlying data during diligence to pressure-test revenue quality. They complement rather than compete.
