The Complete Guide to Buying a SaaS Business

Everything a first-time or experienced buyer needs to evaluate, price, and close a SaaS acquisition — from the initial search through post-close integration.

Buying a SaaS business is the fastest path to recurring revenue ownership — but it's also the easiest way to overpay for a shrinking asset. This guide covers the complete acquisition lifecycle from a buyer's perspective, with specific attention to the diligence signals that separate good deals from bad ones.

1. Sourcing Deals: Where to Find SaaS Businesses for Sale

Marketplaces (Most Accessible)

Acquire.com, Flippa, MicroAcquire, and Quiet Light Brokerage list SaaS businesses from $5K–$10M+ asking prices. Each has different quality filters — Acquire.com tends to have higher-quality listings with verified data rooms.

Direct Outreach (Highest ROI)

SaaS businesses not for sale publicly are often available at the right price. Look for solo-founder businesses, retiring boomer SaaS owners, or lifestyle businesses whose founders have moved on to other projects. Signals: stale blogs, no new features in 12+ months, founder posting on LinkedIn about their "next venture."

Broker Networks

FE International, Dealflow, and Quiet Light Brokerage manage curated deal flows. Their fees (10–15% of deal value) are worth it for first-time buyers who need handholding through the process.

2. Initial Screening: 5-Minute Deal Filter

Before spending hours on diligence, filter deals with three data points you can get from public info or a 5-minute seller call:

  • MRR > $3K for micro-SaaS or > $20K for growth-stage deals — below these thresholds, the business is a side project, not a replacement income
  • Revenue not concentrated in a single customer — ask "what percentage of revenue comes from your top 3 customers?" If >40%, the deal is fragile
  • Founded at least 24 months ago — younger than 2 years and you're buying a prototype, not a proven business

3. Deep Diligence: The 5-Layer Framework

These are the five layers every buyer should investigate, in priority order:

  1. Churn & Retention — Gross logo churn, NRR, cohort retention curves. Request raw subscription data and compute it yourself. Churn Lens does this from a CSV upload.
  2. Concentration Risk — HHI, top-5 share, tier concentration, geo/vertical concentration
  3. Revenue Quality & Decay — MRR trend, organic MRR projection, contraction vs. expansion ratio
  4. Technical & Operational Health — Codebase audit, CI/CD maturity, dependency freshness, single points of failure
  5. Legal & Compliance — IP ownership, ToS assignability, vendor contract change-of-control clauses

4. Valuation: What Multiple Should You Pay?

In 2026, SaaS valuation multiples have compressed from the 2021 peak. Current ranges by deal size:

  • Micro-SaaS (<$5K MRR): 2–3.5× ARR. These are often founder-dependent. Price in transition risk.
  • Small ($5K–$20K MRR): 3–5× ARR. Wide range depends on growth rate and churn.
  • Growth ($20K–$100K MRR): 4–7× ARR. NRR above 100% commands the premium.
  • Scale ($100K+ MRR): 5–10× ARR. Institutional buyers compete here; data quality and team depth matter.

5. Deal Structure: Earn-outs, Holdbacks, and Transition Periods

Most SaaS acquisitions include a transition period where the seller stays on as a contractor (30–90 days for micro-SaaS, 6–12 months for larger deals). Structure notes:

  • Earn-out based on retention: Tie 10–20% of the purchase price to top-customer retention at 12 months post-close
  • Holdback for receivables: Hold back 5–10% for 6 months to cover billing disputes or unexpected churn
  • Transition SLA: The seller must respond within 24 hours during the transition period

6. Post-Acquisition Integration (First 90 Days)

The most common failure point is integration that tries to change too much too fast. The first 90 days should be:

  • Days 1–30: Observe. Don't change pricing, don't redesign the product, don't fire anyone. Map all systems, processes, and vendor relationships.
  • Days 31–60: Stabilize. Fix any broken payment flows, update the billing system, and ensure support channels are operational with your team.
  • Days 61–90: Optimize. Start with the highest-impact change — often pricing or packaging — which the seller under-optimized.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is hidden churn in a SaaS acquisition?

Hidden churn is revenue decay that headline metrics conceal: customers on annual plans who have already stopped using the product, paid accounts sitting inactive, or revenue concentrated in a few logos about to leave. A SaaS business can show flat MRR while its real retention is collapsing. ChurnLens surfaces these signals before you buy, so you price the deal on true revenue quality.

How does ChurnLens score revenue quality?

ChurnLens analyzes five dimensions: revenue concentration, logo retention, annual-plan churn risk, inactive paid accounts, and MRR decline patterns. Each is weighted into a single 0-100 revenue-quality score benchmarked against comparable SaaS businesses. The score tells an acquirer whether reported MRR is durable or propped up by customers who are one renewal away from leaving, all before the deal closes.

Why do SaaS acquirers need due diligence on churn?

Purchase price is usually a multiple of recurring revenue, so overstated retention directly inflates what you pay. A business with 20% hidden annual-plan churn is worth far less than its MRR implies. Buyers who skip churn diligence discover the decay only after closing, when it is too late to renegotiate. ChurnLens gives that visibility during the evaluation window instead.

What red flags should I check before buying a SaaS business?

Watch for revenue concentrated in a handful of accounts, a widening gap between signups and active users, annual contracts that never renew, and MRR that grows only through discounting. Each pattern signals fragile revenue. ChurnLens automatically flags these red flags from uploaded revenue data and ranks them by how much they threaten the durability of the recurring revenue base.

Key terms, defined

Revenue concentration
The share of total revenue coming from the largest customers — high concentration is a churn and valuation risk.
Logo retention
The percentage of customers (logos) retained over a period, independent of expansion revenue.
Net revenue retention (NRR)
Revenue retained from existing customers including expansion and contraction, expressed as a percentage.
