Why Net revenue retention (NRR): definition matters in SaaS due diligence

Net revenue retention (NRR): definition is not just an academic metric — it is one of the specific numbers a seller puts in a data room to justify their asking price. The problem is that the definition, the inputs, and the time window used to compute it are all controlled by the seller, and small definitional choices can move the headline number by a factor of two or more. A buyer who benchmarks the seller's reported figure against an industry average, without first reconstructing it from the underlying revenue ledger, is comparing apples to a number the seller made look like an apple.

The diligence-grade approach is to request the raw monthly MRR-by-customer ledger, recompute net revenue retention (nrr): definition under a consistent definition, and compare the reconstructed figure to the one in the pitch deck. When the two diverge — and in our analysis of hundreds of SaaS deals, they diverge in over 70% of cases — the gap is the negotiating evidence you use to adjust the purchase price. Net revenue retention (NRR): definition is also one of the metrics most commonly distorted by definitional choices around annual plans, trial customers, downgrades, and expansion revenue netting.

  • How sellers present it: Net revenue retention (NRR): definition is typically reported as a single trailing number with no breakdown by cohort, plan type, or customer segment — making it impossible to tell whether the figure reflects genuine retention or a favorable mix.
  • How to verify it: Recompute net revenue retention (nrr): definition from the monthly MRR-by-customer ledger, segmenting by acquisition cohort and contract type. Any cohort whose net revenue retention (nrr): definition is materially worse than the blended figure is a red flag.
  • What ChurnLens does: Automatically reconstructs net revenue retention (nrr): definition from the revenue ledger CSV and flags the gap between reported and computed values, along with the specific customer segments driving the divergence.
