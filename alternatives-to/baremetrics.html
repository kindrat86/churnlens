ChurnLens vs Baremetrics

Baremetrics alternative for buyer-side SaaS due diligence

Baremetrics tracks your own SaaS metrics. ChurnLens stress-tests someone else's before you buy it. Here's when each is the right tool.

Quick verdict

Baremetrics and ChurnLens solve different problems. Baremetrics is operator-side subscription analytics — you connect your own billing and watch your MRR, churn, and LTV as you run your SaaS. ChurnLens is buyer-side due diligence — you upload a target company's raw subscription CSV and get an acquisition risk report and an A–F revenue-quality grade in minutes. If you're evaluating a SaaS to acquire, ChurnLens is the right fit; if you're running your own SaaS, keep Baremetrics.

What Baremetrics does well

Baremetrics launched in 2013 (founded by Josh Pigford, now owned by Xenon Partners) as a Stripe-first subscription-analytics dashboard.

  • Real-time MRR, ARR, LTV, and churn dashboards from a live billing connection (Stripe, Recurly, Chargebee, Braintree, App Store).
  • Recover — automated dunning for failed/expired-card payments.
  • Cancellation Insights and Trial Insights for operators reducing their own churn.
  • Forecasting and public benchmarks for a SaaS you actively run.

Where ChurnLens wins (for acquirers)

Baremetrics assumes you own the account it's watching. In an acquisition you usually get a CSV export from the seller, not their Stripe keys — and Baremetrics reports churn using the account's own configuration, so it inherits the seller's definition of churn rather than stress-testing it.

  • Runs on the raw CSV a seller hands you — no access to their live billing required.
  • Recomputes churn from first principles, surfacing what an operator dashboard configured to the seller's definitions won't: excluded downgrades, early annual cancellations, and inactive-but-billed “zombie” MRR.
  • Scores customer-concentration risk — the single-customer and top-5 dependency that quietly caps a valuation.
  • Returns a benchmarked A–F revenue-quality grade and a ranked red-flag report, not just charts you interpret yourself.
Illustrative example. A seller reports 2% monthly churn; the raw CSV implies closer to 9.4% once excluded downgrades and early annual cancellations are counted. An operator dashboard configured to the seller's own definitions won't flag that gap — a buyer-side recompute will. (Figures are illustrative of the pattern, not Baremetrics's output.)

The key difference

Baremetrics answers “how are my subscription metrics trending?” ChurnLens answers “is this target's revenue as durable as the seller claims, and what will it cost me after close?” One is a dashboard for an operator; the other is a risk report for a buyer. They're complementary, not substitutes — but only one is built for the deal.

Frequently asked questions

Is ChurnLens a Baremetrics alternative?

For SaaS acquisition due diligence, yes. Baremetrics is operator-side subscription analytics for running your own SaaS; ChurnLens is buyer-side due diligence for evaluating one you're about to buy. If you're monitoring your own live metrics, keep Baremetrics; if you're stress-testing a target's numbers before a purchase, ChurnLens is purpose-built for that.

Can I use Baremetrics for SaaS acquisition due diligence?

You can look at Baremetrics's dashboards if you get access to the seller's account, but it reports churn using that account's own settings and needs a live billing connection. It won't recompute hidden churn or hand you a buyer's risk grade.

Does ChurnLens connect to Stripe?

No. ChurnLens works from the raw subscription CSV the seller exports, so you can run diligence on a target without ever touching their live billing credentials.

What does ChurnLens output that a metrics dashboard doesn't?

A benchmarked A–F revenue-quality grade and a ranked red-flag report tuned to acquisition risk — hidden churn, customer-concentration risk, annual-plan decay, and zombie (inactive-but-billed) MRR — recomputed from the raw data rather than reported per the seller's configuration.
